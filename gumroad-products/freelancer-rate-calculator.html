Part 1

Pricing Models Explained

There's no single right way to price. Here are the most common models, when to use each, and how to structure them.

Hourly Pricing

Bill by the hour. Simple and transparent.

+ Easy to calculate, fair for exploratory work

- Punishes efficiency, caps your income, clients watch the clock

Best for: Consulting, ongoing support, early-stage freelancers

Project-Based Pricing

Flat fee for a defined deliverable.

+ Rewards efficiency, predictable for clients, higher perceived value

- Scope creep risk, hard to estimate early on

Best for: Design, development, writing, clear-scope projects

Value-Based Pricing

Price based on the ROI you deliver.

+ Highest earning potential, aligns incentives

- Requires confidence & proof, harder to sell initially

Best for: Marketing, strategy, sales optimization, consulting

Retainer / Subscription

Monthly fee for ongoing access/work.

+ Predictable income, long-term relationships, compounding revenue

- Can become a trap if scope isn't defined

Best for: Social media management, maintenance, ongoing advisory

The Progression Path: Most successful freelancers evolve from Hourly (beginner) to Project-Based (intermediate) to Value-Based (expert). Start where you're comfortable, but always be moving toward value-based pricing.

Part 3

How to Raise Your Rates

Scripts, strategies, and frameworks for confidently increasing your prices without losing clients.

When to Raise Your Rates

  • You're booked solid — if you can't take on new work, your price is too low
  • Everyone says yes — if no one pushes back on pricing, you're underpriced
  • You've improved — new skills, certifications, case studies, or results
  • It's been 6-12 months — annual rate increases are standard practice
  • Your costs have increased — inflation, tools, taxes
  • You're resentful — if you dread client work, pricing is usually the root cause

The 5 Strategies for Raising Rates

Strategy 1: New Clients at New Rates

Easiest approach. Keep existing clients at current rates, quote all new clients at your new rate. Over time, your average rate rises naturally.

Strategy 2: The Grandfather Transition

Give existing clients 30-60 days notice. Let them keep the old rate for 1-2 more months, then transition to the new rate.

Strategy 3: Add Value, Then Raise

Add something new to your offering (faster delivery, extra revisions, monthly report, bonus deliverable), then increase the price. The raise feels justified because they're getting more.

Strategy 4: Repackage Your Offer

Instead of raising the hourly rate, switch to project-based pricing at a higher total. Clients focus on outcomes, not hours.

Strategy 5: The "Premium Tier" Addition

Keep your current offer AND add a premium version at a higher price. Some clients will upgrade; others stay where they are. Revenue increases either way.

Rate Increase Scripts

Script 1: The Annual Increase (Email)

"Hi [Client Name],

I wanted to give you advance notice that starting [date — 30+ days from now], my rates will be adjusting to $[new rate] per [hour/project/month]. This reflects [increased demand for my services / additional certifications I've earned / the enhanced results I'm delivering / industry-standard annual adjustment].

I've genuinely loved working with you on [specific project/achievement], and I'm committed to continuing to deliver exceptional results.

If you have any questions, I'm happy to chat. Thank you for your continued trust in my work.

[Your Name]"

Script 2: The Value-First Approach (Email)

"Hi [Client Name],

I've been reflecting on the results we've achieved together: [specific result 1], [specific result 2], and [specific result 3]. I'm proud of what we've built.

To continue delivering this level of work — and to invest in [new tools / continued education / expanded capabilities] — I'll be updating my pricing to $[new rate] effective [date].

For you as an existing client, I'd like to offer [sweetener: one extra month at current rate / a small bonus / priority scheduling].

Let me know if you'd like to discuss. I'm excited about what's ahead for [their project].

[Your Name]"

Script 3: The In-Person / Call Version

"I wanted to talk about our working arrangement. Over the past [time], we've accomplished [result], and I've invested significantly in [skills/tools/capabilities]. As I continue to grow my practice, I'm adjusting my rates to $[new rate] starting [date]. I wanted to give you plenty of notice and talk through how we can keep this partnership working well. What are your thoughts?"

Then: be silent and let them respond. Don't fill the silence with justifications.

Handling Pushback

If They Say: "That's too expensive"

"I understand. My pricing reflects [the results I deliver / the quality of work / the investment I've made in my skills]. I'd be happy to adjust the scope to fit your budget — perhaps we could [smaller scope option]. But I'm not able to adjust the rate itself, as it's consistent across all my clients."

If They Say: "We found someone cheaper"

"I appreciate you sharing that. There will always be lower-priced options — and some of them might be great. What I can tell you is that my rate reflects [X years of experience, specific results, reliability, and the fact that I deliver on time every time]. If cost is the primary factor, I understand. But if results and quality matter, I'm confident in the value I provide."

If They Ask for a Discount

"I keep my pricing consistent for all clients, so I'm not able to offer a discount on the rate. However, I can [offer a slight scope adjustment / offer a retainer for a lower effective rate / offer a longer-term commitment at a small discount]. What would be most helpful for you?"

The #1 Rule of Pricing: If you raise your rates and lose a client, you now have time to find a client who will pay your new rate. You cannot grow your business by keeping every client at old rates. Some client turnover during a rate increase is healthy and expected.
Part 4

Pricing Psychology & Tactics

Small tweaks to how you present pricing can dramatically change how clients perceive and respond to it.

1. Anchoring

Always present your highest-priced option first. When a client sees a $10,000 option before a $5,000 option, the $5,000 feels like a deal. Without the anchor, it feels expensive.

2. The Rule of Three

Offer three tiers: Basic, Standard, Premium. Most people choose the middle option. Make your preferred (most profitable) option the middle tier.

3. Price Presentation

  • Remove the dollar sign on proposals — "5,000" feels less painful than "$5,000"
  • Use round numbers for premium — $5,000 feels more premium than $4,997
  • Break it down — "$417/month" feels smaller than "$5,000/project"
  • Compare to alternatives — "Less than the cost of a full-time hire at $60K/year"

4. The Confidence Tax

People pay more for confidence. If you present your price with hesitation ("so, um, my rate is... $75 an hour... if that works for you?"), you've already lost. State your price clearly and stop talking. Confidence is worth a 20-30% premium.

5. Frame the Value, Not the Cost

  • Instead of: "My rate is $150/hour" say "The investment for this project is $3,000, and based on similar projects, clients typically see a [X]x return within [timeframe]."
  • Instead of: "This costs $2,000" say "For a $2,000 investment, you'll receive [deliverable] that will [specific outcome]."

6. Payment Terms That Help You

  • 50/50: 50% upfront, 50% on delivery (most common)
  • 100% upfront: Offer a 5-10% discount for full payment upfront (improves cash flow)
  • Milestone payments: 40/30/30 split at project milestones (best for large projects)
  • Retainer + overage: Monthly retainer for a set scope; additional work at hourly rate
