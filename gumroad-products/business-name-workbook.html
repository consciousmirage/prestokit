Section 1

Brainstorming Frameworks

Seven proven approaches to generate dozens of name candidates. Don't filter yourself during brainstorming — write everything down, then evaluate later.

Framework 1: The Mind Map Method

Write your core product/service in the center, then branch out in these directions:

  1. What you do — verbs, actions, processes
  2. Who you serve — audience, demographics, identities
  3. How you make them feel — emotions, aspirations, outcomes
  4. Your values — principles, beliefs, culture
  5. Metaphors — what is your business like? (an anchor? a lighthouse? a rocket?)

Your Mind Map Space

Framework 2: The Word Mashup

Combine two relevant words into one new word. This is how many iconic brands were born.

  • Write 20 words related to your business in Column A
  • Write 20 words related to the feeling/outcome in Column B
  • Combine beginnings of Column A words with endings of Column B words

Examples: Pinterest (Pin + Interest), Instagram (Instant + Telegram), Groupon (Group + Coupon), Microsoft (Microcomputer + Software)

Framework 3: The Foreign Language Method

Translate your key concept words into Latin, Greek, Italian, French, Japanese, or Hawaiian. These often sound sophisticated and are more likely to have available domains.

Free tools: Google Translate, WordHippo, Etymonline.com

Examples: Volvo (Latin: "I roll"), Lego (Danish: "play well"), Audi (Latin: "hear/listen")

Framework 4: The Acronym Builder

Write a phrase that describes your mission, then use the first letters to create a name.

Examples: IKEA (Ingvar Kamprad Elmtaryd Agunnaryd), ESPN (Entertainment and Sports Programming Network), ASOS (As Seen On Screen)

Framework 5: The Modifier + Noun Formula

Pair an unexpected adjective or modifier with a common noun. The contrast creates memorability.

Examples: Red Bull, Blue Apron, Honest Tea, Liquid Death, Kind Bar, Brave Browser

Framework 6: The Origin Story Method

Draw from personal history: founder's name, hometown, street you grew up on, a meaningful place, date, or memory.

Examples: Adidas (Adi Dassler), Mercedes (founder's daughter), Burton (Jake Burton), Versace (Gianni Versace)

Framework 7: The Invented Word Approach

Create a brand-new word that sounds right for your brand. Focus on how it sounds, looks, and feels.

  • Soft sounds (L, M, S, F) = gentle, elegant, premium
  • Hard sounds (K, T, X, Z) = bold, energetic, tech
  • Short (1-2 syllables) = punchy, modern, memorable
  • Longer (3+ syllables) = sophisticated, established, premium

Examples: Kodak, Xerox, Hulu, Etsy, Spotify, Zillow

Section 5

Name Scoring Rubric

Score your top name candidates on each criterion below (1-10 scale). A great business name scores 65+ out of 100. Use this to compare your top 3-5 options objectively.

Criteria What to Evaluate Score (1-10)
Memorability Can someone remember it after hearing it once? Is it catchy?
Pronounceability Can people say it correctly on the first try? No confusion?
Spellability Can people spell it after hearing it? Will they find your website?
Meaning / Relevance Does it connect to what you do, who you serve, or your brand values?
Uniqueness Is it distinct from competitors? Does it stand out in your industry?
Emotional Appeal Does it evoke the right feeling? Does it resonate with your audience?
Scalability Will it still work if you expand products, services, or markets?
Domain Available Is the .com (or suitable alternative) available or affordable?
Social Handles Is the name available (or close) on key social media platforms?
Visual Potential Can you imagine it as a logo? Does it look good in text form?
How to use this rubric: Print multiple copies — one for each name candidate. Score honestly (don't inflate scores for names you're emotionally attached to). Ask 3-5 trusted people to score each name independently, then average the results. The highest-scoring name is usually the right choice.

Section 6

Domain & Social Media Checking Guide

Your name is only good if you can actually use it online. Follow this checklist for every name candidate.

Domain Name Checklist

  1. Check .com availability at Namecheap.com or GoDaddy.com
  2. If .com is taken: Check if it's for sale (often at Afternic or Sedo) and what the asking price is
  3. Alternative extensions to consider: .co, .io, .so, .shop, .studio, .agency, .design — but .com is always preferred
  4. Avoid hyphens and numbers in domains — people will forget them
  5. Check for typo domains — buy common misspellings if you can afford it
  6. Google the domain to see if it was previously used (check Archive.org Wayback Machine)

Social Media Handle Checklist

Check availability on all platforms even if you won't use them all immediately. Claim them now to protect your brand.

  1. Namechk.com — checks username availability across 50+ platforms at once
  2. Instagram — search directly in the app
  3. TikTok — search directly in the app
  4. Twitter/X — search directly on the platform
  5. Facebook — try to create a Page with the name
  6. LinkedIn — create a Company Page
  7. YouTube — search for the channel name
Handle Strategy: If the exact handle isn't available, try: get[name], [name]hq, [name]official, try[name], hello[name], or the[name]. Pick one variation and use it consistently across ALL platforms.

Email Address Checklist

  • Can you get hello@[name].com or similar?
  • Is the domain clean with no spam history? (Check at MXToolbox.com)
  • Set up Google Workspace or similar for professional email
Section 7

Trademark Basics

Protect your name legally. This is not legal advice — consult a trademark attorney for formal guidance. But here's what every business owner should know.

Step 1: Search Before You Commit

  • USPTO TESS Search (tmsearch.uspto.gov) — search the U.S. trademark database for free
  • Google the name + your industry to find unregistered common-law trademarks
  • Check state trademark databases (varies by state)
  • Search business entity databases in your state's Secretary of State website

Step 2: Understand Trademark Strength

Trademarks are ranked by strength (strongest to weakest):

  1. Fanciful — Invented words (strongest): Kodak, Xerox, Exxon
  2. Arbitrary — Real words, unrelated meaning: Apple (computers), Amazon (retail)
  3. Suggestive — Hints at qualities: Netflix, Airbus, Pinterest
  4. Descriptive — Describes the business (hard to protect): "Quick Print," "Best Pizza"
  5. Generic — Cannot be trademarked: "Computer Store," "Pizza Place"
Key Insight: Aim for Fanciful, Arbitrary, or Suggestive names. They're easiest to trademark and hardest for competitors to challenge. Descriptive names are difficult and expensive to protect.

Step 3: File Your Trademark

  • Cost: $250-$350 per class to file with the USPTO
  • Timeline: 8-12 months for approval
  • DIY vs. Attorney: You can file yourself, but an attorney reduces the risk of rejection (typical cost: $500-$1,500 in addition to filing fees)
  • Use TM immediately: You can use the TM symbol as soon as you start using the name in commerce. The (R) symbol is only after official registration.

Common Trademark Mistakes to Avoid

  • Not searching before committing to a name
  • Choosing a descriptive name that can't be protected
  • Assuming a domain purchase = trademark rights (it doesn't)
  • Ignoring businesses in other states with the same name
  • Waiting too long to file — someone else might file first
